The Popularization of Her Theory

Marie Meurdrac, the art of making science popular

How did people talk about science in the 17th century? Long before popularization became a genre in its own right, a woman, Marie Meurdrac, had the intuition that chemistry needed to be made accessible to everyone.

To do this, she abandons technical jargon in favor of clear explanations and examples drawn from everyday life.

Democratize elite knowledge

In the 17th century, science is a matter for specialists. Treatises are written in Latin, in coded language. Marie Meurdrac takes the opposite view of this trend.

To do this, she abandons technical jargon in favor of clear explanations and examples drawn from everyday life.

The recipe for a good explanation

Her method is based on simple principles: start with the concrete, illustrate each notion with an experiment or recipe, and never dissociate theory from practice.

Then, she uses analogies and comparisons to make abstract phenomena more intelligible.

"I have tried to be so clear and intelligible that it will be almost impossible not to understand me, if one reads what I write with a little application."

Marie Meurdrac,
La chymie charitable et facile, en faveur des dames (1666)

A feminist vision of pedagogy

By choosing to address women, Marie Meurdrac does more than make an audience choice: she poses a political act.

Her pedagogy is therefore inseparable from her feminist commitment. She wants to give women confidence, prove to them that they have every place in the world of science.
