The Shadow Figures

Released in 2016, the film Hidden Figures revealed to the general public an unknown but essential story of the space conquest: that of three African-American mathematicians whose calculations sent the first American astronauts into space. A powerful story about perseverance, discrimination and historical rehabilitation, which directly echoes Marie Meurdrac's path.

Those who aimed for the moon

Early 1960s, in the midst of the Cold War. The United States are engaged in a frantic space race against the Soviet Union. At the heart of NASA, a group of African-American women, nicknamed the "colored calculators", perform in the shadows the complex trajectory calculations.

The film follows the destiny of three of them: Katherine Johnson, a calculation genius capable of verifying the trajectories of Apollo missions; Dorothy Vaughan, who fights to become NASA's first black supervisor and anticipates the computer revolution; and Mary Jackson, who has to fight in court to have the right to take engineering courses in a high school reserved for whites. The film highlights their indispensable role in the successes of the American space program, a role that remained invisible for a long time.

The double wall of discrimination

The fight of these three women is double. They must not only prove themselves as scientists in an ultra-competitive environment, but also face the double discrimination of being women and being black in a segregated America.

"Every time we try to advance, they move the finish line. Every time."

Mary Jackson

The film powerfully illustrates the daily humiliations: separate toilets and cafeterias, impossibility to access certain meetings, contempt from their white male colleagues. But in the face of adversity, Katherine, Dorothy and Mary show unflinching determination. Their perseverance, solidarity and excellence end up breaking barriers one by one, forcing respect and recognition.

From segregated Virginia to the Sun King's France

The parallel between the heroines of Hidden Figures and Marie Meurdrac is striking. Three centuries apart, these women shared the same fight: that of legitimacy in a scientific world that denied them access and recognition.

Like Katherine Johnson who had to prove the validity of her calculations, Marie Meurdrac had to justify her claim to "teach" chemistry. Like Dorothy Vaughan who had to self-train to master new computers, Marie Meurdrac had to create her own laboratory to conduct her experiments. And like Mary Jackson who had to fight for her right to education, Marie Meurdrac advocated for knowledge to be accessible to all women.

These destinies remind us that the "Matilda Effect" is not a fatality, but a historical construction that courageous women have, at all times, sought to dismantle.

"Separate and equal is not the same thing. It's not because things are that way that they are right."

Dorothy Vaughan

The film is an adaptation of the non-fiction book by Margot Lee Shetterly, Hidden Figures: The American Dream and the Untold Story of the Black Women Who Helped Win the Space Race. It was acclaimed by critics and received three Oscar nominations, including Best Picture.
