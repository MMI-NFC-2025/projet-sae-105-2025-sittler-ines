Charitable and Easy Chemistry
in Favor of Ladies

The book that opened science to women

In 1666, a singular work was published in Paris. Its title, La chymie charitable et facile, en faveur des dames, is a promise: to make science accessible to a female audience hitherto kept away from knowledge.

A manual for all

Paris, 1666. In the bookstores of rue des Billettes and rue du Piastre, a book attracts attention. Far from the complex grimoires reserved for a male elite, Charitable Chemistry aims to be a practical and understandable guide.

Its objective is twofold: to heal the poor with inexpensive preparations and to allow women to appropriate useful knowledge for their daily lives.

Structure of the work

The book is divided into six parts: the first exposes the basic principles and operations; the second deals with plants; the third with animal substances; the fourth with minerals; the fifth is devoted to the manufacture of medicines.

Science as emancipation

More than a simple collection of recipes, Marie Meurdrac's book is a plea for female intelligence. She defends a science at the service of humanity.

For her, the goal is not to turn lead into gold, but to relieve suffering and embellish life.

A modern legacy

Marie Meurdrac's approach astonishingly prefigures contemporary scientific popularization. Her insistence on experimentation and observation announces the modern scientific approach.

Today, while the place of women in science remains a major issue, her work reminds us that the fight for equality also goes through the sharing of knowledge.

"I thought that charitable chemistry would be useful to women, and that the recipes it contains would allow them to relieve the sufferings of the people."

Marie Meurdrac,
Preface of La chymie charitable et facile, en faveur des dames
