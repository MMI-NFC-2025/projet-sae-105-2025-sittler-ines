Marie Meurdrac

the chemist who wanted to give science back to women

In the 17th century, a woman stood up against the prejudices of her time to impose her passion for chemistry. Marie Meurdrac was not only a pioneer in a man's world: she advocated for knowledge to become a right accessible to all. Portrait of an inspiring figure, whose legacy still resonates today.

A vocation born in the shadows

Mandres-les-Roses, around 1610. In this town near Paris, Marie Meurdrac is born into a family of notables. Her father, Vincent Meurdrac, is a notary and clerk. Her sister, Catherine, will become Madame de La Guette and leave Memoirs to posterity. But Marie chooses a more discreet and infinitely bolder path: chemistry.

At a time when this discipline still oscillates between alchemy and modern science, when women have no access to universities or learned academies, she sets up a personal laboratory. Married to the captain of Grosbois castle, she has the material stability necessary to conduct her experiments.

"Minds have no sex"

In 1666, Marie Meurdrac takes a step forward. She publishes La chymie charitable et facile, en faveur des dames, a 334-page work that will become the first chemistry treatise written by a woman. In her preface, she anticipates criticism with disarming lucidity.

But she sweeps away these objections with a famous formula: "Minds have no sex." For her, if women had access to the same education as men, they could equal them. Her book is therefore not only a chemistry manual. It is a feminist manifesto ahead of its time.

"Minds have no sex; and if those of women were cultivated like those of men, they could equal them."

Marie Meurdrac – 1666

A legacy for posterity

Success is immediate. Reprinted several times in France, translated into German and Italian, the work testifies to the relevance of her approach. Drawing on the theories of Paracelsus but also on her own experience, Marie Meurdrac proposes a concrete, useful and demystified science.

More than a simple compilation of knowledge, her book is an invitation to practice, an open door to a world hitherto forbidden. Today, Marie Meurdrac is recognized as a major figure in the history of science.

"I flattered myself on the other hand that I am not the first who have put something under the Press; that Minds have no sex."

Marie Meurdrac,
La chymie charitable et facile, en faveur des dames (1666)
